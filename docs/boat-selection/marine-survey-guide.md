# Marine Survey Guide

## Why Survey

A marine survey is the single most important step before purchasing a boat. For a liveaboard purchase where you're committing your housing to this vessel, it's even more critical. The cost of two surveys ($1,200-$2,000 total for a 35-footer) is trivial compared to missing a $15,000 problem.

## Certifying Organizations

### SAMS — Society of Accredited Marine Surveyors
- Founded 1980s, based in Jacksonville, FL
- Designated by USCG as an Accepted Third Party Organization
- **Accredited Marine Surveyor (AMS)** requirements:
  - Minimum 5 years surveying experience within past 10 years
  - Submit sample surveys for review
  - Pass written exam in specialty
  - Continuing education every 2 years to maintain accreditation
- Specializations: Hull & Machinery, Cargo, Engines, Commercial Workboat, Yachts & Small Craft

### NAMS — National Association of Marine Surveyors
- 14 regions (11 domestic US, 2 Canadian, 1 European)
- **Certified Marine Surveyor (NAMS-CMS)** requirements:
  - 5 years experience in marine industry
  - Relevant degree or Merchant Marine License
  - Pass CMS Technical Examination
  - Specialization exams: Yacht & Small Craft, Hull & Machinery, Cargo
- Three membership levels: Apprentice → Associate → Certified CMS

### Key Point
Anyone can call themselves a marine surveyor. The certifications and experience are what matter. Always use a SAMS AMS or NAMS CMS surveyor.

## Types of Survey

### Pre-Purchase Condition & Valuation Survey
- Most thorough — what you want when buying
- Operational testing of all systems and equipment, in and out of water
- Includes haul-out for below-waterline inspection
- Final report: current condition, necessary repairs, market value
- Required by most lenders and insurance companies

### Insurance Survey
- Covers same tests and evaluations as pre-purchase
- Can be done afloat or ashore
- Focused on insurability and risk assessment

### Damage Survey
- After collision, grounding, or severe weather
- Assesses cause and extent of damage
- Works with repair shops for cost estimates

## Two-Survey Strategy (Recommended)

### Survey 1: Full Condition & Valuation
- SAMS AMS or NAMS CMS surveyor
- Covers hull, deck, rigging, systems, safety equipment
- Includes haul-out
- **Note**: Marine surveyors are generally NOT qualified engine surveyors

### Survey 2: Dedicated Engine Survey
- Separate diesel mechanic specializing in marine engines
- Compression test, oil analysis, injector testing, cooling system evaluation
- Surveyor will likely recommend this anyway for anything beyond basic evaluation
- This is your de facto "second opinion" on the most expensive and critical system

### Optional: Actual Second Full Survey
- Not common, but not unreasonable for a liveaboard purchase
- Two competent surveyors can have meaningfully different opinions
- If reports disagree significantly on something structural, the disagreement itself is information
- Total cost of two full surveys still trivial compared to a missed major problem

## What the Surveyor Examines

### Hull & Structure
- Fiberglass condition (tap test for delamination/water intrusion in deck core)
- Keel-to-hull joint (any movement or cracking?)
- Hull-to-deck joint construction and condition
- Rudder bearing play
- Chainplates (where rigging meets hull — often corroded under deck, invisible from outside)
- Through-hull fittings condition
- Osmotic blistering below waterline

### Deck & Topsides
- Soft spots (water has wicked into core through old screw holes)
- Hardware bedding (leaking deck hardware = water damage)
- Stanchion bases and lifeline condition
- Hatches and portlight sealing

### Rigging
- Standing rigging: broken strands, corrosion, swage fitting condition
- Age of rigging (if unknown, assume it needs replacement)
- Mast step, chainplates, turnbuckles
- Running rigging condition

### Systems
- Engine operation (basic — detailed evaluation from engine surveyor)
- Electrical system condition, wiring quality
- Plumbing (freshwater, head, through-hulls)
- Bilge pump operation
- Steering system
- Ground tackle (anchor, windlass, rode)

### Safety Equipment
- Fire extinguishers (current, accessible)
- Navigation lights
- PFDs
- Flares (not expired)
- VHF radio

## What to Do With the Survey

1. **Read it thoroughly** — the surveyor's report is your maintenance roadmap
2. **Negotiate**: Major findings reduce the purchase price or seller fixes before closing
3. **Prioritize repairs**: Safety items first, then structural, then systems, then comfort
4. **Insurance**: Your insurer may require specific repairs before coverage
5. **Baseline**: The survey becomes your reference document for the boat's condition at purchase

## Survey Costs

| Service | Approximate Cost (35' boat) |
|---|---|
| Full condition & valuation survey | $600-$1,000 |
| Haul-out for survey (yard fee) | $350-$500 |
| Engine survey (diesel mechanic) | $250-$500 |
| **Total** | **$1,200-$2,000** |
