# Boat Selection Guide

## Target Specifications

- **Size**: 33-35 feet (sweet spot for singlehanding, liveaboard comfort, and coastal capability)
- **Type**: Coastal cruiser or bluewater cruiser, monohull
- **Era**: 1982-1995 preferred (golden era of production fiberglass sailboats)
- **Budget**: $15,000-$25,000 purchase price
- **Must have**: Transferable slip with liveaboard permission

## Why 33-35 Feet

- Large enough for comfortable liveaboard (standing headroom, real galley, functional head, adequate tankage)
- Small enough for confident singlehanding with modern equipment
- Adequate seaworthiness for coastal California conditions
- Fits within budget at used prices
- Manageable maintenance burden
- Standard travel-lift haul-out, no special arrangements needed

## Singlehanding Requirements

Modern equipment has pushed the comfortable singlehanding limit to 40-45 feet. At 35 feet with proper setup:

- **Roller furling headsail** — furl/unfurl from cockpit
- **In-mast or in-boom mainsail furling** — or lazy jacks with single-line reefing
- **Self-tailing winches** — handle lines without a second pair of hands
- **Autopilot** — essential for singlehanding; maintains course while you handle sails or lines
- **Bow thruster** — transforms singlehanded docking from terrifying to manageable
- **Electric windlass** — mandatory for singlehanded anchoring at this size

## Categories to Ignore

- **Racing boats**: Deep narrow hulls, spartan interiors, no liveaboard comfort
- **Daysailers/weekenders**: Undersized systems (tanks, electrical, plumbing) for living aboard
- **Motorsailers**: Heavy, undersized rigs, frustrating to actually sail
- **Anything under 30'**: Too cramped for comfortable liveaboard; limited systems capacity

## Recommended Brands by Tier

### Top Tier (if budget allows: $25-30k)
- **Pacific Seacraft** — Heavy layup, offshore-capable, designed by Bill Crealock in SoCal for these exact waters. Loyal following, excellent resale.
- **Island Packet** — Exceptional build quality, great engine access, full keel protection. Excellent liveaboard boats.
- **Tartan** — Sparkman & Stephens designs, solid construction, good sailing performance.
- **Bristol** — Overbuilt for the era, heavy displacement, great sea boats.

### Solid Middle Tier (primary search range: $15-25k)
- **Catalina 34/36** — The workhorse of American coastal cruising. Huge owner community, readily available parts, decades of collective knowledge about every quirk. The Catalina 34 is one of the most popular liveaboard boats on the west coast.
- **Ericson 34** — Well-regarded California-built boats, good sailing performance.
- **Pearson 33/35** — Solid New England construction, good offshore reputation.
- **Hunter 34/35** — Wide availability, spacious interiors for the size.

### European Production (available, generally fine)
- **Beneteau** — Common on used market, build quality varies by era
- **Jeanneau** — Similar to Beneteau
- Parts can be slightly harder to source

## Decade Guide

### 1970s ($5k-$15k)
- Cheap and plentiful
- Some overbuilt (early Tartan, Bristol, Pearson), some underbuilt
- Dark, cramped interiors by modern standards
- Electrical systems minimal and usually completely redone (quality varies)
- If still floating and maintained, hull is proven
- Nearly every system replaced at least once, often by amateurs

### 1980s ($15k-$30k) ← SWEET SPOT
- Builders had mastered fiberglass but hadn't started cutting costs aggressively
- More livable interiors, better ventilation, more practical galleys
- More robust electrical systems
- Reliable engines (Yanmar and Westerbeke hit their stride)
- Best price-to-quality ratio for liveaboard buyers

### Early-to-mid 1990s ($25k-$50k)
- Better deck hardware, improved interior joinery
- Beginning of modern electrical systems with proper panels and circuit protection
- More refined overall, but pricier
- Less common at $20-25k

## What to Evaluate on Specific Boats

### For Liveaboard
- Standing headroom in cabin (6'+ preferred)
- Galley size and layout (can you actually cook?)
- Head compartment (room to exist; shower sump working?)
- Ventilation (hatches, dorades, portlights — critical in San Diego heat)
- Storage volume
- Electrical panel capacity and space for expansion

### For Modernization Plans
- **Electrical panel**: Older boats may have 8-circuit panels. You'll need 20+ circuits for modern systems. Evaluate upgrade difficulty.
- **Battery bank space**: Where will 400-600Ah of lithium physically go?
- **Engine access**: Some boats are famous for terrible access (certain Catalina/Universal diesel combos). Since you'll do your own maintenance, this matters enormously.
- **Solar panel mounting**: Is there an arch, bimini frame, or rail space for 300-400W of panels?
- **NMEA 2000 backbone routing**: Where will cables run?

### For Seaworthiness
- Keel type (see [Seaworthiness doc](seaworthiness-and-heavy-weather.md))
- Cockpit volume and drain capacity
- Companionway closure strength
- Hull-to-deck joint construction
- Portlight/window size and strength

### For General Condition
- Deck core moisture (tap test for soft spots)
- Chainplate condition (where rigging attaches — often corroded under deck)
- Through-hull fittings (bronze dezincification?)
- Rudder bearing play
- Keel-to-hull joint (any movement?)
- Stuffing box drip rate

## Shopping Strategy

1. **Search criteria**: 33-36', 1982-1995, coastal/bluewater cruiser, under $25k, with transferable slip
2. **Primary sites**: YachtWorld, Boat Trader, Sailboat Listings, Craigslist
3. **Join owner forums** for any model that interests you — an hour reading the Catalina 34 forum teaches more than any broker will
4. **Let the survey do the heavy lifting** on individual boats — your job is to pick a good model, the surveyor evaluates the specific example
5. **Prioritize**: boat + slip + liveaboard permission as a package deal

## Key Principle

> A well-loved Catalina will outperform a neglected Pacific Seacraft every time, and it'll be more pleasant to live on too. Condition and maintenance history matter more than brand.
