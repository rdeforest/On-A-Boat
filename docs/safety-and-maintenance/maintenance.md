# Maintenance Guide

## Maintenance Ratio

- Neglected older boat: ~10 hours maintenance per 1 hour sailing
- Well-sorted boat: ~3-5 hours maintenance per 1 hour sailing
- Liveaboard advantage: constant sensory feedback about boat condition (can't ignore a weeping through-hull when the bilge is under your bed)

## Annual Rhythm

### Bottom Work (annually or every 18 months)

**Haul-out process**:
1. Schedule with boatyard (Driscoll's, Koehler Kraft, or Shelter Island yards in San Diego)
2. Travel lift picks boat up, moves to jack stands on the hard
3. Pressure wash bottom
4. Scrape, sand, and repaint with antifouling paint
5. Inspect and replace zinc anodes
6. Inspect through-hulls, prop, shaft, rudder, cutlass bearing
7. Any below-waterline repairs while you can see everything
8. Splash back in

**Between hauls**: Diver scrubs bottom every 1-2 months (San Diego warm water grows fast)

**Cost**: $1,500-$2,500 per haul including a week of yard storage, paint, and materials (DIY labor)

### Zinc Anodes (every 6-12 months)
- Sacrificial zinc on hull, prop shaft, rudder
- Corrode instead of your expensive metal parts
- Check at every haul-out, replace when half consumed
- Cheap parts, important job, easy to forget

### Topside (annually)
- Clean and wax gelcoat
- Inspect all deck hardware for signs of leaking
- Re-bed any hardware showing water intrusion (water through screw holes is the silent killer of fiberglass boats)
- Clean and lubricate winches

### Rigging
- **Standing rigging inspection**: Annual. Look for broken strands, corrosion, cracks at swage fittings.
- **Standing rigging replacement**: Every 10-12 years. **Not optional.** Cost: $3,000-$8,000. Failure = losing the mast (catastrophic, dangerous).
- **Running rigging**: Replace individual lines as they show chafe, UV damage, or stiffness.

## Engine Maintenance

### Regular Service
| Task | Interval | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Oil change | 100 hours or annually | Whichever comes first |
| Oil filter | With every oil change | |
| Fuel filters (Racor) | 200 hours or as indicated by vacuum gauge | Carry many spares |
| Raw water impeller | 200 hours or 2 years | Carry spare; practice 5-minute change |
| Drive belt(s) | Inspect at oil change; replace annually or when worn | Carry 2 spares |
| Coolant | Check level regularly; flush/replace per manufacturer | |
| Zincs (heat exchanger) | Annually | Separate from hull zincs |
| Hoses | Inspect annually; replace when stiff or cracked | |
| Fuel biocide | Every fill-up | Prevent biological contamination |

### Fuel System
- **Diesel that sits grows biological contamination** (algae, bacteria) that clog filters and kill the engine
- Treat fuel with biocide with every fill-up
- Change Racor filters regularly (watch vacuum gauge)
- Run fuel polishing system regularly
- Run engine often enough that fuel doesn't sit for months
- Visual inspection of Racor bowl for water accumulation

### Common Marine Diesel Brands
- **Yanmar**: Reliable, well-supported, most common in 1980s+ boats
- **Westerbeke**: Similar reliability, good parts availability
- **Universal**: Adequate, but some models notoriously difficult to access in certain hulls

## Systems Maintenance

### Freshwater System
- Tank cleaning/sanitizing annually
- Inspect hoses and clamps for deterioration
- Water pump rebuild kit on hand
- Water heater anode (if equipped)

### Head (Marine Toilet)
- Entire maintenance universe unto itself
- Source of the most unpleasant boat repair stories
- Rebuild kit on hand at all times
- Joker valves wear out — learn to replace
- Use only marine-grade toilet paper
- Pump-out regularly (San Diego Bay is no-discharge)

### Electrical System
- Battery bank monitoring (via Victron BMS — see monitoring doc)
- Connection inspection (corrosion is constant in salt environment)
- Shore power cord and inlet inspection
- Bilge pump testing (monthly, manually)

### Through-Hulls
- Exercise (open and close) quarterly
- Inspect at haul-out
- Check for dezincification on bronze fittings
- Keep wooden plugs sized to each through-hull accessible

## Haul-Out Logistics

For a 35-foot keel sailboat, trailering is impractical (12,000-16,000 lbs, 10-12' beam = oversize load). Use a boatyard travel lift.

| Item | Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Haul and launch (per foot, each way) | $10-$15/ft |
| 35' boat haul + launch | $700-$1,050 |
| Yard storage (daily/weekly) | Varies; ~$100-$200/week |
| Bottom paint (materials, DIY) | $400-$800 |
| Zincs | $50-$150 |
| Pressure wash | Often included with haul |
| **Total typical annual haul** | **$1,500-$2,500** |

## Spare Parts Kit

### Always On Board
- Engine oil (enough for one change)
- Oil filter
- Fuel filters (Racor elements — multiple)
- Raw water impeller + gasket
- Drive belts (2)
- Coolant (premixed)
- Fuel biocide
- Hose clamps (assorted sizes)
- Spare hose (assorted sizes)
- Zincs (prop shaft, rudder)
- Through-hull wooden plugs
- Head rebuild kit / joker valves
- Electrical: fuses, wire, connectors, terminals, heat shrink, electrical tape
- Sealant: 3M 4200, 3M 5200, silicone
- Duct tape, rigging tape
- Stainless steel hardware assortment (screws, bolts, nuts, washers)
- WD-40, penetrating oil, winch grease
- Flashlight batteries
- Sail repair kit (palm, needles, thread, sail tape)

### Nice to Have
- Spare alternator
- Spare water pump
- Spare bilge pump (manual, small)
- Epoxy repair kit (for emergency hull/deck repair)
