# Ground Tackle & Anchoring

## Anchor Windlass

### Why It's Essential
Hauling a 35-pound anchor with chain rode by hand is exhausting, slow, and dangerous if you lose your grip when the boat pitches. An electric windlass is essentially mandatory for singlehanding a 33-35' boat.

### Types

**Horizontal windlass**: Motor mounts below deck, only the gypsy (chain-gripping wheel) protrudes through deck. Cleaner look, better weather protection, more complex installation.

**Vertical windlass (capstan)**: Mounts on deck with motor below. More common on production boats, easier to install and service.

### Key Components
- **Gypsy**: Chain-gripping wheel — must be matched exactly to your chain size
- **Drum**: On same shaft, for rope rode (if using combination rope-and-chain system)
- **Foot switch**: On foredeck, operated by foot while handling anchor
- **Helm remote control**: Essential for singlehanding — control anchor from helm while managing boat position with engine

### Specifications
- Power draw: 80-150 amps under load (requires heavy-gauge cable run to battery bank)
- Requires reinforced foredeck structure underneath
- Dedicated circuit breaker
- Brands: Lewmar, Quick, Maxwell, Muir
- Cost: $1,500-$3,000 installed for 33-35' boat

### Selection Tip
If the boat you buy already has a windlass, that's a significant bonus. Add to evaluation checklist.

## Hull Protection

### The Danger Zone
When the anchor comes up through the last few feet and breaks the surface — it's hanging from the chain, swinging with boat motion, and can bang against hull or bow roller fitting.

### Solutions

**Proper bow roller**: Fitting at the bow tip that the chain runs over. Anchor self-stows by pulling up into a shaped cradle. Must be matched to your anchor type (CQR, Delta, Rocna each sit differently). A well-designed bow roller positions the anchor clear of the hull.

**Anchor platform (bowsprit)**: Extends forward of hull, keeping anchor and chain entirely away from gelcoat. Some boats have these as standard; can be added aftermarket.

**Hull protectors**: Rubber or plastic guards mounted on hull sides near the bow in the "strike zone."

**The windlass itself**: Pulls anchor at a controlled, steady rate — much less swinging than hand-over-hand hauling. The last few feet (anchor transitioning from water to bow roller) happen smoothly and predictably.

## Chain Counter

A sensor that counts chain links as they pass the gypsy. Tells your monitoring system exactly how much chain is deployed.

### Integration with Signal K
- Chain deployed + GPS position + depth data → calculated swing radius
- Dashboard shows current anchor status
- **Anchor drag alarm**: Alerts if GPS position drifts beyond calculated swing radius
- One of the most popular Signal K plugins
- Phone buzzes at 3 AM if boat is dragging → deal with it before you're on the rocks

## Anchoring Basics

### Scope
Ratio of rode (chain/rope) deployed to water depth plus height of bow above water.

| Conditions | Minimum Scope |
|---|---|
| Calm, settled weather | 5:1 |
| Normal conditions | 7:1 |
| Heavy weather | 10:1+ |

Example: 15' water depth + 5' bow height = 20'. At 7:1 scope, deploy 140' of chain.

### Anchor Types for Coastal California
- **Rocna/Mantus**: New-generation anchors, excellent holding in sand and mud (common San Diego Bay bottom)
- **CQR (plow)**: Traditional, good all-around, common on cruising boats
- **Delta**: Similar to CQR, self-launching from bow roller
- **Danforth/Fortress**: Excellent holding in sand/mud, folds flat for storage. Good secondary anchor.

### Secondary Anchor
Always carry a second anchor on separate rode. Different type from primary if possible (different anchors perform differently in different bottoms). Deployable independently for:
- Backup if primary drags
- Two-anchor set in tight anchorages or strong current
- Kedging off if grounded (deploy from dinghy, winch boat toward anchor)

## San Diego Anchoring Regulations

- San Diego Bay is **no-discharge** — holding tank pump-out required
- La Playa Cove: 72-hour, weekend-only anchorage (max 40 boats)
- A5 anchorage: Glorietta Bay
- A9 anchorage: Requires vessel inspection by Harbor Police, permit in person
- All anchorages require permit
- Effective January 1, 2025: Safe harbor anchorage relocated from La Playa Cove to Glorietta Bay Basin

## Lessons Learned (from Previous Boat)

1. **Drop anchor before reaching shore** if losing control — counterintuitive when instinct says "get away from shore," but stops the situation from getting worse while you figure out your next move
2. Kelp-fouled prop makes engine useless — in kelp-heavy areas, monitor prop effectiveness continuously
3. A working windlass would have made recovery from the grounding scenario significantly more manageable
4. Tidal miscalculation + kelp + non-functional inboard = the compounding failure cascade that the redundancy philosophy exists to prevent
