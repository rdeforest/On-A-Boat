# Propulsion

## Impellers vs Propellers

### Why big ships use propellers
Propellers accelerate a large disc of water a small amount. Impellers (jet drives) accelerate a small volume of water a large amount. Same thrust, but power required goes up with the square of velocity change. Moving a lot of water slowly is fundamentally more efficient. Same principle as high-bypass turbofans replacing turbojets in aviation.

### Where impellers win
- **Safety**: No exposed spinning blade. Jet boats/jet skis, rescue craft, river boats.
- **Draft**: Operate in shallow water.
- **Maneuverability**: Vector thrust for steering, no rudder needed.
- **High-speed crossover**: Above 30-40 knots, propellers cavitate badly. Impellers manage cavitation better at speed. Fast ferries and military vessels use waterjets for this reason.

### Why sailboats use propellers
- Sailboat motors operate at low speed, low power, intermittently — impellers' least efficient regime
- **Drag under sail**: A folding/feathering prop streamlines when not in use. A waterjet intake duct is a permanent hole creating turbulence.
- Waterjet intakes clog with kelp/debris (harder to clear than prop fouling in many cases)
- Limited fuel capacity makes 20-30% efficiency penalty unacceptable

## Folding & Feathering Props

### Folding props ($1,500-$3,000 for 2-blade; $2,500-$5,000 for 3-blade)
- Blades fold backward like a closed flower when not in use
- Dramatically reduced drag under sail (feel the difference in light air)
- Fouling material tends to slide off when folded
- Brands: Flexofold, Gori

### Feathering props ($3,000-$6,000+)
- Blades pivot edge-on to water flow
- Similar drag reduction, slightly different mechanism
- Blades can rotate on axis, so heavy fouling less likely to stay wound tight
- Brand: Max-Prop (premium option)

### Recommendation
For a coastal cruiser doing real sailing (not just motoring between marinas), a folding or feathering prop is one of the highest-value upgrades available. Get the engine sorted first, then this.

## Diesel Engine Reliability

### Making the primary engine bulletproof (instead of adding a backup)
There's no practical place for a second inboard on a 35' monohull. The strategy is to address each failure mode:

**Fuel contamination (most common failure)**
- Dual Racor fuel filter system with manifold valve — switch to clean filter without stopping engine
- Carry many spare filters
- Fuel polishing system (continuous circulation and filtration)
- Biocide treatment religiously
- These three measures eliminate ~70% of engine failures

**Cooling system failure (second most common)**
- Carry spare raw water impeller — practice changing in under 5 minutes
- Carry spare cooling hoses and hose clamps in every size
- Spare alternator belt(s)

**Electrical failure**
- Battery selector switch to start engine from house bank if start battery dies
- Carry spare alternator

**Transmission/shaft**
- Rarer, harder to fix at sea
- Know your engine intimately

### Common marine diesels
- **Yanmar** — reliable, well-supported, common in 1980s+ boats
- **Westerbeke** — similar reliability, good parts availability
- **Universal** — adequate but some models have notoriously poor engine access in certain hulls (check before buying!)

## Backup Propulsion Options

### Primary backup: Sails
You're on a sailboat. Learning to sail into an anchorage or even a slip under sail alone is the ultimate redundancy. Old-school skill, deeply satisfying, means the engine is a convenience not a dependency.

### Secondary backup: Emergency outboard
- Small outboard bracket on stern rail
- Compact 6-8 HP outboard stored in cockpit locker
- Won't push a 35-footer fast, but gives 2-3 knots to reach harbor
- Think of it as the spare tire — not primary use, better than nothing
- Very different from previous experience of outboard as primary propulsion on a 30-footer

### Not recommended
- Second inboard (no room, impractical)
- Electric trolling motor (insufficient power for sea conditions)

## Defouling Techniques

- **Reverse prop briefly**: Change in rotation can unspool wrapped kelp/line
- **Folding prop advantage**: When folded, material slides off smooth profile
- **Serious fouling**: Still requires going over the side with a knife
- **Prevention**: Awareness of kelp fields, seaweed, floating lines; avoid areas with known fouling hazards (Point Loma kelp beds)
