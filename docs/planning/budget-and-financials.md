### Budget Discipline

- The $60k emergency fund exists to absorb the unknown unknowns of older boat ownership: soft decks, corroded fuel tanks, chainplate failures, etc.
- Resist the urge to let it creep into the purchase. A $35k boat is not a $20k boat plus $15k of emergencies that haven't happened yet.
- The $20k repair budget should go to meaningful upgrades (electronics, automation, folding prop, windlass) — not to foundational work that should already be done on a $20k boat.

## $10k vs $30k Boat Analysis

### $10k boat + $30k repairs (NOT recommended)
- Typically a 1970s-80s production boat with decades of deferred maintenance
- First $15-20k consumed by foundational safety work: engine rebuild ($5-8k), rigging replacement ($3-6k), electrical rewire ($2-4k), plumbing ($1-2k)
- High discovery cost — opening up neglected systems reveals cascading problems
- 6-12 months on the hard before it's livable
- Risk of ADD-driven loss of motivation during extended restoration

### $20-25k boat + $15-20k upgrades (RECOMMENDED)
- Well-maintained 1980s-90s cruiser with running engine, serviceable rigging, functional systems
- Upgrade budget goes to fun, quality-of-life improvements
- Move aboard within weeks of purchase
- Learn what the boat actually needs through daily use

### $30k boat + $10k upgrades
- Best condition, least work, fastest to livable
- May stretch purchase budget uncomfortably close to emergency fund territory
- Good option if a boat-with-slip deal at this price appears

## Cash Flow Considerations

- Boat expenses are **lumpy**, not smooth. Some months: $50. Some months: $800 parts weekend.
- Maintain a dedicated boat maintenance fund with monthly contributions, draw as needed.
- Annual haul-out is a predictable $1,500-$2,500 lump — budget for it.
- The emergency reserve covers $5,000-$10,000 surprises that come at bad times.
